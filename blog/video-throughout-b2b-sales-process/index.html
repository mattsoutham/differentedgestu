The companies closing the most B2B deals have one thing in common: video in the sales process, not just the marketing.

Most businesses treat video as a top-of-funnel asset. The research suggests this is leaving conversion on the table at every stage of the funnel — not just the first one.

Video in the sales process isn't a new idea. What has changed is the evidence for how early, how consistently, and at how many stages it compounds.

Start at the website

According to data from Wyzowl (2025), 83% of video marketers report that video has directly increased sales. HubSpot's research puts the conversion lift from adding video to a landing page at up to 86% compared to pages without it.

Websites using video see an average conversion rate of 4.8% — significantly above the 2–3% benchmark for B2B sites without it (Vidico, 2026). The homepage isn't just a brochure. For most buyers, it's the first qualifying decision: do I trust this company enough to find out more?

Move into the sales sequence

Research on short-form video lead magnets shows view-to-opt-in rates of 61.4% on mobile — substantially above the rates achieved by static alternatives (Martal, 2026). When sales reps send a video rather than a wall of text, response rates rise. Prospects who have watched the company explain itself — not read about it — arrive to discovery calls with context already built.

LinkedIn amplifies this at scale

Video posts on LinkedIn receive five times more engagement than static content (LinkedIn, 2024). LinkedIn video uploads increased 34% year-on-year in 2024.

For B2B businesses, this matters because four out of five LinkedIn members are involved in business decisions. The sales cycle on many B2B deals is long — video keeps the company visible and the relationship warm across the months between first contact and signed contract.

The internal sales training argument

Research from MySalesCoach and Aircall found that sales reps who rate their coaching as excellent or very good are 50% more likely to hit or exceed quota. Only 27% of reps currently hit quota.

The fastest path to changing that number isn't more meetings — it's capturing how the top performer thinks and sells, making it watchable, and making it standard.

The companies already proving it

HubSpot, Salesforce, and LinkedIn have all built video-first content strategies as part of their own sales and marketing operations. Not just because they sell to marketers, but because it converts. HubSpot has publicly reported that adding video to its landing pages produced meaningful lifts in lead generation and product trial sign-ups. LinkedIn's own thought leadership ad format — video-led, executive-fronted — produces up to 45% brand lift and is seeing 12% year-on-year growth in advertiser adoption.

The pattern is consistent

Video doesn't just help at one stage. It helps at every stage — and the effect compounds when you use it consistently rather than occasionally.

Buyers need to trust before they buy. They need to understand before they trust. And they need to be reminded, repeatedly, across the weeks and months of a sales cycle, that your business is worth their attention. Text can do some of that. Video does all of it — faster, at greater depth, with a retention rate that written content can't match.

The question for most scaling businesses isn't whether to add video to the sales process. It's why they're waiting.
